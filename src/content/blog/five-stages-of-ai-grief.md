# The Five Stages of AI Grief — A Developer's Obituary

<details style="margin: 1.5rem 0;">
<summary style="cursor: pointer; padding: 0.5rem 0;"><strong>TL;DR</strong> — for developers already using AI to summarize articles</summary>
<div style="margin-top: 1rem;">

| Stage | Symptom | Tell-tale phrase |
|---|---|---|
| **Denial** | Insisting AI is "just autocomplete" | "It can't reason, it predicts tokens" |
| **Anger** | Aggressive LinkedIn posts | "It halluccinates. It's not conscious. I read a paper." |
| **Bargaining** | Pivot to prompt engineering | "Actually I'm an *AI-augmented* developer now" |
| **Depression** | Existential paralysis | "What's the point of learning anything" |
| **Acceptance** | Shipping faster than ever | Quietly using Cursor, never mentions it publicly |

In 1969, Swiss-American psychiatrist **Elisabeth Kübler-Ross** published *On Death and Dying*, in which she described the five emotional stages that terminally ill patients move through when confronting their mortality. It is a profound, compassionate work about the human condition.

In 2023, developers used her framework to process the arrival of **ChatGPT**.

This is their story.

---

## Stage 1 — Denial

> "It's just autocomplete. A fancy autocomplete."

The first response to a threatening reality is to **make it smaller**. If the thing can be diminished, it cannot threaten.

And so the refrain began. *It doesn't actually understand code. It predicts the next token based on statistical patterns in training data.* This is technically true, in the same way that saying a piano "just hits strings with small hammers" is technically true. Accurate. Illuminating. And completely beside the point.

The denial came in **several flavors**:

- **The Philosophical Denial**: "It's not *conscious*. It doesn't *know* what it's doing." Correct. It also doesn't know it just replaced your Tuesday afternoon.
- **The Technical Denial**: "It makes mistakes. I tested it on a tricky regex and it failed." Yes. So do junior developers. We keep them around anyway.
- **The Scope Denial**: "It can only do simple tasks." This one had a half-life of approximately **six weeks per model release**.
- **The Regurgitation Denial**: "It just spits back what it was trained on. It's not *creating* anything." This one was popular. It's also the argument that, taken seriously, applies to every human being who has ever learned a language. You too were trained on input data. You too produce outputs shaped entirely by what you absorbed. The difference between you and someone who speaks Mandarin is not intelligence — it's **training data**. If "learning from examples and recombining them" disqualifies an entity from doing real intellectual work, the indictment is considerably broader than intended.

The denial was understandable. When a technology arrives that can perform, in seconds, a task you spent **years mastering**, the mind's first move is not acceptance — it's reclassification. *What I do is different. What I do is deeper. What I do is real.*

Some developers in this stage began citing Chomsky. This is how you know the denial has become **load-bearing**.

---

## Stage 2 — Anger

> "The hype is insane. These people have never written real software."

Denial, when it fails, becomes **anger**. And the anger was magnificent.

LinkedIn became a theater of grievance. Long posts appeared, authored by engineers with titles like "Senior Staff Principal Architect," explaining in careful detail why **AI cannot replace developers**. The posts cited papers. They included disclaimers. They used the phrase *"to be fair"* before saying something deeply unfair.

The anger had targets:

- **The AI companies**, for "stealing" training data, for overpromising, for disrupting an industry that had been disrupting everyone else for thirty years without notable remorse.
- **The non-technical enthusiasts**, who were using these tools *wrong* and drawing *incorrect conclusions*.
- **The other developers** who were excited, who were shipping things with AI assistance, who were not sufficiently outraged. These were called "naive." Or worse: "vibe coders."

The most telling form of anger was the **"it hallucinates"** argument. This became a sort of password among the faithful — a shibboleth to identify the serious engineers from the credulous masses. *Yes but it hallucinates.* Said with great gravity. As though this single fact was a complete rebuttal to an entire technological shift.

It reminded me, structurally, of two other communities: **Java developers** and **Jehovah's Witnesses**. Both have an elaborate, internally consistent worldview. Both knock on your door to share it. Both are absolutely certain that if you just **read the right material**, you would agree with them. And both are, in their own way, waiting for something that may not arrive in the form they expect.

**Anger is not argument.** But it is information. What the anger was actually saying, underneath the papers and the LinkedIn posts, was something simpler: *I am afraid of becoming irrelevant.* That is a human thing to feel. It deserved a more honest address than it got.

---

## Stage 3 — Bargaining

> "I'm not a developer anymore. I'm an *AI-augmented engineer*."

When anger exhausts itself, the mind seeks a **deal**.

The bargaining stage of AI grief produced some of the most creative rebranding in the history of job titles. Developers who had spent years insisting AI was a gimmick began, quietly, updating their LinkedIn headlines. Not "Software Engineer." **"AI-Augmented Software Engineer."** Not "Developer." **"Prompt Engineer."**

This was survival instinct dressed as strategy, and there is nothing wrong with it — except the dishonesty about the motive.

The bargaining also produced **new hierarchies**. If the machine could write code, then what mattered was knowing *what* to build. Strategy. Architecture. *Taste*. The developers who had once looked down on product managers began emphasizing that they, too, understood the product. The ones who had dismissed "soft skills" now spoke warmly of **communication** and **leadership**.

None of this was wrong, exactly. These things do matter. But the sudden discovery of their importance, timed precisely with the arrival of tools that threatened to commoditize raw coding ability, had the feel of a man who discovers he loves his wife the week she announces she's leaving.

Some bargaining took a **purer form**: the belief that by learning AI tools faster than everyone else, one could stay ahead of the wave. Become the person who wields the tools rather than the person replaced by them. This is probably correct. It is also, structurally, a form of bargaining — the implicit contract being: *if I adapt fast enough, the disruption won't reach me personally.*

Reasonable. **Not yet acceptance.** But closer.

---

## Stage 4 — Depression

> "What's the point of learning anything if it's all going to be automated anyway?"

The bargaining fails. Or succeeds, but the success doesn't feel like enough. And then comes **the silence**.

Depression in the AI grief cycle looked like **paralysis**. Developers who had been voracious learners — always picking up a new language, a new framework, a new paradigm — went quiet. *What's the point?* If GPT-5 was coming, if agents were coming, if the whole stack was going to be generated end-to-end within a few years, then why spend 40 hours learning Rust? Why go deep on distributed systems? Why **become anything**, if anything you become can be approximated?

This was the most honest stage, and the most painful, because it pointed at the **real wound**.

The developers who suffered most in this stage were not the weakest engineers. They were often the most dedicated. The ones who had built their **entire sense of self** around the identity of being a developer. Who had absorbed the culture — the memes, the Stack Overflow karma, the GitHub contribution graphs — as a complete worldview. For whom being a good engineer was not just a job but a **moral category**.

When that identity is threatened, the question isn't *will I find another job*. The question is: **who am I if I'm not this?**

That is a hard question. And unlike a bad deploy, you cannot hotfix it.

---

## Stage 5 — Acceptance

> "I shipped more last week than I did in the previous month."

Acceptance is not **resignation**. This is a common confusion.

The developers who reached acceptance did not conclude that AI had won and humans had lost. They did not stop caring about code quality, or system design, or engineering craft. They did not become "vibe coders" who blindly ship generated slop and call it a product.

They simply **stopped making the tool's existence about themselves**.

And here is where I want to introduce a piece of philosophy disguised as a trip to the grocery store.

When you buy a **chicken escalope at the supermarket**, you did not raise the chicken. You did not slaughter it, butcher it, or portion it. You did not develop the refrigeration supply chain that kept it viable for transport. You did not design the packaging. You benefited from a vast infrastructure of human and mechanical labor, absorbed the output, and made dinner.

This does not make you a **fraud**. It does not make you inferior to a hunter-gatherer who brought down the animal himself and knows every step of the process. It makes you a **modern human** operating in a division-of-labor economy that has, by almost every metric, made life longer, less painful, and more interesting.

The question was never: *did you do it yourself?* The question was always: *what did you make with it?*

**The only permanent thing in the world is its impermanence.** Every tool we've treated as definitional — the compiler, the IDE, the framework, the package manager — was, at some point, the thing that was going to "make real programmers obsolete." Every generation of engineers stood at this threshold and found, eventually, that the threshold was not a wall but a door.

The developers who made peace with AI did not abandon their craft. They **expanded the boundary of what they could make**. They kept the judgment, the taste, the architectural sense, the knowledge of what systems fail and why — and they stopped spending their Tuesdays on boilerplate.

**Identity is not a job title.** It never was. The engineers who thrived were the ones who had always known this — who were curious first, and credentialed second. Who built things because they found it meaningful, not because it made them members of a guild that could not be automated.

You were never the keyboard shortcuts. You were never the typing speed. You were never the fact that you could name every HTTP status code from memory.

You were the **judgment**. And judgment, for now, remains stubbornly human.

---

## Postscript — A Note on What Comes Next

The five stages are not a ladder with a final rung. **Acceptance is not a destination.** The technology will keep moving, and with each movement, there will be new versions of denial, new LinkedIn posts, new bargaining.

The healthy response is not to have no reaction. It's to **recognize the reaction for what it is** — a healthy immune system trying to protect something real — and then ask whether the thing being protected is the skill, or just the story about the skill.

The skill can adapt. The story needs to go.

---

*The developer who spends their energy proving AI cannot replace them is already being replaced — by the developer who spent that time building something.*
